# Global Quantum & AI Commons (GQAC) — v0.1 Draft

**Epistemic Status: CONJECTURED**
All claims in this document are tagged per the GoS Rung Rule. Nothing here is proved or demonstrated. This is a governance conjecture with identified precedents.

## 1. Core Thesis

Quantum computing and AI verification infrastructure should be governed as a global public commons, not as proprietary national assets. The GQAC conjectures that the institutional model proved by CERN for particle physics can be adapted for quantum + AI cooperation, with formal verification transparency (Glassbox methodology) as the epistemic accountability layer.

**Kill condition:** If no viable institutional host or member-state interest materializes within a defined timeline, the conjecture is retired.

---

## 2. The CERN Precedent — PROVED

CERN demonstrates that large-scale, multi-nation, publicly funded scientific infrastructure works. The relevant proven facts:

- 23 member states with binding governance and cost-sharing
- Open-access publication mandate for publicly funded research
- Infrastructure invented for knowledge sharing (the World Wide Web, 1989)
- Decades of sustained operation across political transitions
- The model scales: from the original 12 founding states (1954) to 23 members plus associate and observer states

CERN is the existence proof that the governance pattern is viable. It does not prove that the pattern transfers to quantum + AI. That transfer is the conjecture.

---

## 3. The CERN Open Quantum Institute — MOTIVATED

The CERN Open Quantum Institute (OQI) extends the CERN governance model specifically to quantum technologies. It provides the closest existing institutional bridge between the CERN precedent and the GQAC conjecture.

The OQI's mandate — applying quantum computing to global challenges under open, multilateral governance — motivates the claim that CERN-style cooperation is not limited to particle physics but can generalize to quantum infrastructure.

**GQAC's relation to OQI:** GQAC does not compete with or duplicate the OQI. It conjectures a broader commons that integrates quantum infrastructure with AI verification governance. The OQI is the most relevant existing model for how such governance could be structured.

## 5. GQAC Architecture (Conjectured)

### 5.1 Three Pillars

1. **Quantum Infrastructure Commons** — Shared quantum computing and networking resources governed under open-access mandates, modeled on CERN's infrastructure sharing.

2. **AI Verification Commons** — Formal verification tools and methodologies (including Glassbox XAI) as public goods, ensuring that AI systems used in quantum research are auditable and transparent.

3. **Governance Protocol** — Multi-stakeholder decision-making modeled on CERN's council structure, adapted for the dual quantum + AI domain.

### 5.2 Glassbox Methodology as Transparency Layer

The Glassbox methodology provides the epistemic accountability mechanism:

- All AI contributions credited with provenance chains
- Multi-model workflows are auditable (which model produced what, verified by which compiler)
- Retractions documented as methodology evidence, not deleted
- The principle: "the compiler is the credential" — formal verification, not institutional authority, determines validity

This addresses a gap in the CERN model: CERN's openness applies to results and publications, but the GQAC conjecture extends openness to the reasoning process itself.

### 5.3 Quantum Stellarator Cooperation Protocol — CONJECTURED

Integration point for international quantum cooperation frameworks. The stellarator precedent (ITER/Wendelstein 7-X) demonstrates that even extraordinarily complex, multi-decade physics engineering projects can sustain international cooperation. The protocol conjectures analogous cooperation structures for quantum infrastructure deployment.
